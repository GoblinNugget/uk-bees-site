Different Bee Species Of The UK

This is a small page on the different species of bees in the UK and some short descriptions for each one.

Here are the most common ones:

Honey Bee

Apis Mellifera

The honey bee (Apis mellifera) is a highly organized and industrious insect known for its crucial role in pollination and honey production. These social bees form intricate colonies, with a single queen, female worker bees, and male drones. Their distinctive striped appearance, with bands of black and amber or brown, makes them easily recognizable. Equipped with specialized structures like pollen baskets and a proboscis for collecting nectar, honey bees tirelessly visit flowers, facilitating cross-pollination. The collected nectar is transformed into honey through a process of regurgitation and evaporation within the hive. Honey bees are essential for agriculture and ecosystems, showcasing the delicate balance between their communal efforts and the vital services they provide to the environment.

White Tailed Bumblebee

Bombus lucorum

The white-tailed bumblebee (Bombus lucorum) is a medium-sized, robust bee characterized by its distinctive white or creamy-colored tail. These social insects are commonly found in Europe and Asia, often seen buzzing around gardens, meadows, and agricultural areas. With a fuzzy, black thorax and abdomen, their name is derived from the conspicuous white hairs at the base of their abdomen. White-tailed bumblebees play a crucial role in pollination, visiting a variety of flowers to collect nectar and pollen. Their colonies are annual, initiated by a queen in spring, and reaching peak activity during the warmer months before declining in autumn. Observing their diligent foraging and distinctive markings adds a touch of nature's charm to any outdoor setting.

Common Carder Bee

Bombus pascuorum

The common carder bee (Bombus pascuorum) is a medium-sized bumblebee species found across Europe and parts of Asia. Recognizable by its dense and gingery fur, it often has a black abdomen with a distinctive white tail. These bees are vital pollinators, visiting a variety of flowering plants to feed on nectar and collect pollen. Common carder bees are known for their ability to hover and navigate complex floral structures with agility. They typically build their nests in grassy areas, utilizing moss, grass, and other plant materials. These social bees live in colonies with a queen, workers, and male bees, contributing to the ecological balance through their crucial role in pollination.

Red Tailed Bumblebee

Bombus lapidarius

The red-tailed bumblebee (Bombus lapidarius) is a striking and widely distributed bumblebee species found in Europe. Its distinguishing feature is the vibrant red coloration at the base of its abdomen, which contrasts with the rest of its black body. The queen, workers, and males of this species share this distinctive appearance. Red-tailed bumblebees are efficient pollinators, visiting a diverse range of flowers to feed on nectar and collect pollen. They are known for their ability to fly in cooler temperatures, making them active early and late in the day. These social bees typically build their nests underground, using abandoned rodent burrows or other suitable cavities. Their colonies, initiated by a mated queen, contribute significantly to pollination dynamics and ecosystem health.

Tree Bumblebee

Bombus hypnorum

The tree bumblebee (Bombus hypnorum) is a distinctive bumblebee species characterized by its unique coloration and nesting habits. Recognizable by its combination of a ginger thorax, black abdomen, and a white tail, this bee stands out in appearance. Originally native to Europe, the tree bumblebee has expanded its range and can now be found in various urban and rural habitats. It is an early forager, visiting a wide array of flowers for nectar and pollen. Unlike many other bumblebees, tree bumblebees often nest in elevated locations, such as bird boxes, tree cavities, or even in residential spaces. This adaptable species plays a crucial role in pollination, contributing to the biodiversity of flowering plants and supporting ecosystem health.

Red Mason Bee

Osmia Bicornis

The red mason bee (Osmia bicornis) is a solitary bee species, known for its vibrant reddish-brown coloration and robust build. With a length of around 8 to 14 millimeters, these bees are relatively small. They are proficient pollinators, playing a vital role in orchards and gardens. As solitary bees, each female builds and provisions her own nest, typically in pre-existing cavities such as hollow plant stems or insect burrows. The name "mason bee" reflects their behavior of using mud to construct partitions within their nests. Red mason bees are active during the spring, displaying an impressive foraging efficiency as they visit various flowers to collect pollen and nectar. Their solitary nature, coupled with their gentle demeanor, makes them fascinating contributors to ecological biodiversity.

Tawny Mining Bee

Andrena fulva

The tawny mining bee (Andrena fulva) is a solitary bee species known for its distinctive appearance and behavior. With a reddish-brown or tawny-colored fur coat, these medium-sized bees are often found in gardens, meadows, and other open habitats. Tawny mining bees are efficient pollinators, collecting nectar and pollen from a variety of flowering plants. As solitary bees, each female constructs her own underground nest, typically in well-drained soil. They are essential pollinators for early spring-flowering plants, emerging early in the season. These bees are non-aggressive and rarely sting, making them welcome visitors to gardens where they contribute to biodiversity and play a vital role in pollination.

Hairy-Footed Flower Bee

Anthophora plumipes

The hairy-footed flower bee (Anthophora plumipes) is a distinctive solitary bee species notable for its striking appearance and behavior. With a predominantly black body and a covering of orange-brown hairs on its hind legs, it is easily identifiable. This bee species is active during the spring and early summer, playing a crucial role in pollination. Hairy-footed flower bees are known for their agile flight and remarkable hovering abilities, allowing them to visit a diverse range of flowers to collect nectar and pollen. They often nest in soft mortar or sandy banks, creating individual burrows. As non-aggressive pollinators, these bees are valuable contributors to the ecosystem, enhancing plant diversity and supporting overall environmental health.
